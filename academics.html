Overview, "Advice", and Lecture Notes

Although I did graduate with a dual degree in the end, I made a couple of missteps along the way from which I wish others can learn. This might be relevant if you're trying to do computational finance and computer science as well.

Most BSCF majors take the core sequence 21-270, 21-370, and 21-420 in their freshman and sophomore years (like I did). In my opinion this unnecessarily front-heavy schedule prevents one from fully appreciating the deep ideas of risk-neutral pricing, backward induction, and stochastic calculus. Stochastic calculus (Brownian motion, Ito integrals, Change of measure...) is especially mind-boggling without some understanding of Riemannian analysis (not to mention Lebesgue and measure theory, which is what probability and hence math finance are all about). However, because most people join the BSCF program during their sophomore fall semester, one may fear that they are losing out if they haven't taken 21-270 and 21-370 by their sophomore fall. Indeed, it is impossible to be accepted for BSCF without taking 21-270, but one can also apply in their sophomore spring semester (or even later!).

So what is one to do? One option is to front-load the other necessary math classes as well. Ideally, 21-355 (Real I) should be taken before 21-420, so probably sophomore fall is a good time (the prospect of taking Real Analysis as a freshman is extremely daunting). 21-325 (Probability) and 21-260 (Differential Equations) are prerequisites for 21-420, so there is no getting around them either. If one also wants a CS major/ degree, they must also keep up with the CS core classes. So that's about 10-12 technical classes in the first three semesters. Not impossible, but certainly challenging.

The other option is to defer 21-270 to sophomore spring, and then take 21-370 and 21-420 during one's junior year. This allows one to comfortably clear the math prerequisites and even take a few interesting math classes (e.g. 21-301, 21-373, other 21-3xx or 21-4xx classes) during the first two years. Particularly capable math majors can consider finishing both undergraduate Real Analysis classes (21-355, 21-356) in their sophomore years. Then they can venture further with the graduate classes 21-720 (Measure and Integration) and 21-721 (Graduate Probability). By then, most of the intellectual nuance and beauty of 21-420 should be apparent. Alas, I never got as far as 21-7xx myself, but many other undergraduates have. The primary reason for that is that the BSCF and CS program all demand attention to other core classes, hindering my pursuit of pure math (I only had time for that in junior year, a pity). Which is why it might be advisable even to aim for a pure math rather than BSCF major. It's frankly more work, but employers recognize and appreciate that. Most BSCF graduates enter the sell-side; buy-side and prop shops mostly hire pure math and/or MS graduates.

By the way, if you're interested in some handwritten lecture notes of math classes, you can find them here. I was not the best math student, but the algebra and analysis classes represented were some of the most stimulating and rewarding I have taken at CMU. Knowing why there's a quadratic formula and extensions to the cubic and quartic cases, but no general formula for quintic equations is one of the most exhilarating things I have experienced in school. Essentially, some of the roots to this equation, and many others, are hidden so far within the depths of the complex plane that no number of elementary numerical operations and square roots can get you there. There is simply no way to write them out exactly.

Activity Descriptions

15-251 Tutor

15-251 is the introductory course in Theoretical Computer Science at CMU. It introduces concepts such as Turing Machines, Decidability, Graph Theory, P vs NP, and Cryptography. As a tutor, I met 1-on-1 with a student every week to review that week's content and reinforce his understanding of the key concepts.

SSA Treasurer

As the treasurer of the Singapore Students Association, I took full responsibility of the organization's finances. A large part of my work was formulating a $6,000+ annual budget and overseeing its disbursement throughout the year. We organized team-building activities, celebrated local festivals, and promoted Singaporean culture to the wider community.

Moneythink Mentor

During my stint at Moneythink CMU as Mentor Captain, I conducted weekly 1-hour mentorships in different Pittsburgh-area High Schools on financial management. We regularly delivered Powerpoint presentations and shared academic materials. Topics covered include Savings, Credit Cards, Loans, Taxes, Investment, College Admissions, and Insurance.

Consulting Club

I served as a Pro-Bono Lead in 2019 at CMU's Undergraduate Consulting Club, participating in 2 pro-bono consulting projects. We liaised with a multinational Healthcare startup for an ongoing project on Indian and U.S. market research. Previously, we also aided a Pittsburgh-based educational non-profit’s sponsor acquisition efforts in 2018-19 by evaluating potential partners and creating a long-term framework for sponsorship retention.

Technical Enhanced Facilities Operator

As a TEF Operator, I performed Level 1 technical support to resolve software issues faced by users of Carnegie Mellon’s Windows-, Mac- and Linux-based computing terminals for 8+ hours per week during term time.

Rowing

I was part of CMU's Club Rowing team during my first Fall semester. The sport taught me much about grit, team chemistry, and coordination, and I enjoyed watching my blade graze the calm waters. Unfortunately, I decided to quit due to physical exhaustion and busier subsequent semesters.
